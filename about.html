Our Solution

1. Geoanalysis and Bioprospecting

Before establishing the microalgae cultivation system, it is essential to perform a thorough geoanalysis and bioprospecting process to identify the optimal location and select the most suitable microalgae strains. The geoanalysis involves surveying potential sites near coastal regions to assess factors such as climate, available land, access to seawater, and proximity to infrastructure. The goal is to find a location that can naturally support rapid algae growth with minimal energy inputs. Hot, dry desert environments near upwelling ocean currents are often ideal, as they provide the necessary sunlight, temperature, and nutrient-rich seawater. Concurrently, the bioprospecting team filters through thousands of local microalgae strains to identify the ones that best fit the use case. Selected species need to be well-adapted to the local climate, can thrive in the available seawater conditions, and have desirable properties for other applications. By using native algae species, the risk of introducing invasive organisms is minimized.

2. Growing Algae — Lab to Greenhouse

After selecting the location and constructing necessary infrastructure, the first step is to grow an algal starter monoculture in a controlled laboratory environment. This is where the key initial conditions are carefully managed to kick-start the rapid "bloom" phase of algae growth. In the lab, factors like temperature, light exposure, nutrient levels, and CO2 concentration are precisely regulated to optimize the growth of a specific microalgae species. This allows the algae to enter an explosive yet sustainable growth phase with the population doubling daily, enabling the algae culture to be scaled up in the next step. Furthermore, to ensure a smooth transition for the algae, they will first be transferred to a greenhouse. The greenhouse will provide more natural environmental conditions, while still offering some additional protection and control compared to outdoor ponds. By doing this, the organisms will gradually adapt to the environment in the outside ponds. This helps maximize the overall biomass yield and productivity of the process.

3. Algae Growth In Ponds

After the initial growth in the lab and greenhouse, the microalgae is transferred to large outdoor ponds. These ponds contain seawater, rich with nutrients and CO2 for the algae to grow, that is pumped directly from nearby coastlines, providing a natural environment for the algae. Combined with the large surface area, exposure to sunlight, CO2, and nutrients, the algae will grow at an exponential rate. As a positive side-effect, the cultivation of microalgae in these open ponds will help enhance the alkalinity of the ocean water, which is an effective strategy for addressing ocean acidification. As the microalgae photosynthesize and remove CO2 from the seawater, they increase the pH and thus the alkalinity of the water. The removal of CO2 reduces the concentration of carbonic acid and increases the concentration of bicarbonate and carbonate ions. This increased alkalinity helps to neutralize the excess acidity in the oceans caused by the absorption of anthropogenic CO2 emissions from the atmosphere. Over time, it will help mitigate the detrimental effects of ocean acidification on marine ecosystems. In order to monitor the pond conditions such as pH levels, temperature, and nutrient levels needed for algal blooms are consistent, sensors are deployed. This is crucial to maintaining the maximum growing rate for algae.

4. Filtration And Drying

When the ponds reach a desired level of algae concentration, motor-powered filtration systems will separate all the algal biomass from the water using specialized membranes, creating a thick "slurry" of the harvested algae. After the algae is removed, the filtered water is returned to the ocean. The water is now de-acidified, which helps restore the local marine ecosystem's biodiversity and contribute towards solving ocean acidification. In the meantime, harvested algae will be pumped up a drying tower – approximately 10 stories high – and sprayed down from the top as a mist. As the algae float downwards through the tower, it will come in contact with the hot, dry air. The heat and low humidity of the air cause rapid evaporation of the water in the biomass slurry. Finally, when they reach the ground, the biomass will take the form of a dry, salty powder, making them easy to store and transport. This dry-spraying process is highly efficient and effective at the same time. Locating the drying tower in a hot, dry desert climate minimizes the energy required for drying by harnessing the natural environmental conditions. Furthermore, spray drying can preserve the nutritional and functional properties of the microalgae biomass.
